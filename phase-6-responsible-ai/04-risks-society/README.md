# Risks & Society

**Phase 6 — Module 4** | Tag: `core` | Type: `theory only`

> You are writing code that will affect people who will never see it, can't audit it, and can't opt out. The Nuremberg defense — "I just wrote the software" — doesn't hold up. But neither does paralysis. So what does responsibility actually look like?

---

## What this module covers

This is the final module of the course. You now know how models work, how to build with them, and how they can fail. This module asks the harder question: what are the consequences for people who never touch your code?

The scope is deliberately broad: misinformation, surveillance, autonomous weapons, job displacement, the existential risk debate, and what responsible practice actually looks like for a developer. Some of these topics have clear answers. Most don't. The goal is not to give you a script but to give you a framework for thinking through the tradeoffs — because you will face them.

---

## Learning objectives

By the end of this module you should be able to:

- Explain why deepfake detection is structurally behind deepfake generation, and what this implies
- Describe how LLMs amplify misinformation differently from previous technologies
- Describe the core legal and ethical debate around autonomous weapons and lethal AI
- Explain what the Clearview AI case demonstrates about AI and privacy
- Describe what the research evidence says about AI-driven job displacement (not the pundits)
- Explain the digital divide in AI access and who benefits most
- Accurately summarise the Yudkowsky, LeCun, and Hinton positions on existential AI risk — without straw-manning any of them
- Compare the EU, US, and Chinese approaches to AI regulation
- Describe what developer responsibility looks like in practice — beyond "don't build harmful things"
- Explain the AI winter scenario and whether current AI development is structurally different from previous cycles
