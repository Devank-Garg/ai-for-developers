# Responsible AI Practice

**Phase 6 — Module 2** | Tag: `core` | Type: `theory only`

> The COMPAS algorithm predicted recidivism with the same accuracy as untrained humans. The problem wasn't accuracy — it was that it got the wrong answers for the wrong reasons, and nobody could explain why.

---

## What this module covers

You understand how alignment works at the model level. This module covers what responsible AI looks like at the system and organisation level — the practices, frameworks, and tools that translate alignment principles into something you can actually audit, document, and improve.

The topics here are not abstract ethics. They are increasingly legal requirements, procurement criteria, and liability questions. The EU AI Act is in force. Regulators are investigating algorithmic systems. Understanding bias, explainability, auditing, and governance is now part of shipping AI responsibly.

---

## Learning objectives

By the end of this module you should be able to:

- Distinguish between allocative and representational bias and explain why they require different responses
- Describe the COMPAS case and explain why two mathematically correct analyses reached opposite fairness conclusions
- Explain what disparate impact means and why intent is irrelevant to legal liability
- Describe what LIME and SHAP do and when explainability is legally required
- Explain what model cards and datasheets are and why they matter for accountability
- Describe the key steps in an AI audit and how slice-based analysis detects bias
- Explain how feedback loops amplify initial bias in deployed systems
- Describe the EU AI Act's risk tiers and what they require of developers
- Apply the NIST AI Risk Management Framework structure to an AI product
- Describe at least three practices for responsible AI with a small team and limited resources
