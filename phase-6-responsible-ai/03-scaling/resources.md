# Resources: Scaling

None required — the concepts file covers everything needed for this module. These are for going deeper.

---

## Essential watching

**"Scaling Hypothesis" — Gwern Branwen**
The most thorough treatment of the scaling hypothesis and its implications. Written not as a paper but as an evolving essay; accessible and comprehensive.

**"AI and the Future of Work" — Daron Acemoglu (MIT, various talks)**
Acemoglu's economic analysis of AI and labour. Sceptical of techno-optimist narratives; grounded in economic data. Good counterweight to industry-produced forecasts.

**"The Dawn of Large Language Models" — Sebastian Raschka (YouTube)**
Technical walkthrough of scaling laws with concrete examples. Developer-oriented; assumes familiarity with ML basics.

---

## Illustrated guides

**"AI and Compute" — OpenAI Blog**
OpenAI's 2018 analysis of the relationship between compute and AI capability over time. Shows the exponential growth curve. The original source for many "AI doubles every X months" claims.

**"Chinchilla's Wild Implications" — lesswrong.com (gwern)**
Clear explanation of what the Chinchilla findings actually imply for AI development trajectories. Non-technical sections are accessible.

---

## Conceptual reading

**"Scaling Laws for Neural Language Models" — Kaplan et al. (OpenAI, 2020)**
The original scaling laws paper. Introduction and results sections are accessible without deep ML background.

**"Training Compute-Optimal Large Language Models" — Hoffmann et al. (DeepMind, 2022)**
The Chinchilla paper. Demonstrates that GPT-3-size models were undertrained. Abstract and discussion sections are sufficient.

**"Emergent Abilities of Large Language Models" — Wei et al. (2022)**
The definitive treatment of emergence in LLMs. Shows the phase-transition patterns across 150+ tasks. Results figures are self-explanatory.

**"An Open Letter on AI Risk" — various signatories (FLI, 2023)**
The open letter calling for a pause on large training runs. Read the letter itself and the responses — both the critical responses and the supportive ones. This is what the public debate looks like.

**"Release Strategies and the Social Impacts of Language Models" — Solaiman et al. (2019)**
OpenAI's analysis of their staged GPT-2 release. A case study in how one lab thought about the open vs. closed decision.

## Practical reference

**AI Index Report — Stanford HAI (annual)**
Stanford's annual report on AI progress. Covers capability benchmarks, compute trends, economic data, and policy developments. The most comprehensive publicly available overview.

**Epoch AI (epochai.org)**
Research organisation tracking AI compute trends, training costs, and capability benchmarks. More rigorous than most public sources. Their compute tracker is useful for understanding the scale of current training runs.

**"The Semiconductor Landscape" — CSET (Georgetown)**
Analysis of the semiconductor supply chain relevant to compute governance. Understanding chip exports requires understanding where chips are made. CSET produces the most rigorous public analysis.
