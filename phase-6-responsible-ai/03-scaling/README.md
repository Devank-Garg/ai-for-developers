# Scaling

**Phase 6 — Module 3** | Tag: `core` | Type: `theory only`

> GPT-2 to GPT-3 wasn't just more capability — models at 100B+ parameters could suddenly do arithmetic and write code. Nobody predicted this. Nobody knows exactly when the next jump happens or what new capabilities it brings.

---

## What this module covers

You've learned how models are trained and how to deploy them responsibly. This module covers what happens as scale increases — not just to model capabilities, but to risk profiles, infrastructure dependencies, economic concentration, and governance challenges.

Scale is not a linear multiplier. It introduces qualitatively new phenomena: emergent capabilities that appear unpredictably, competitive dynamics that resist safety investment, and geopolitical stakes that didn't exist when AI was a niche research field. Understanding these dynamics changes how you think about the systems you're building and the industry you're building in.

---

## Learning objectives

By the end of this module you should be able to:

- Explain what scaling laws are and what the Kaplan et al. findings predict about AI capability
- Describe emergent capabilities and give three concrete examples of abilities that appeared at scale
- Explain why capability unpredictability is a safety problem, not just a research curiosity
- Describe the infrastructure concentration in frontier AI and its implications
- Explain how compute governance works and why geopolitics has become AI policy
- Estimate the environmental cost of training and inference at scale
- Describe the race dynamics that create structural incentives against safety investment
- Explain the open vs. closed model debate and the strongest arguments on each side
- Describe how economic concentration in AI differs from previous technology platform dynamics
